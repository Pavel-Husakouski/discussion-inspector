# Discussion Tactics Analysis

## Alice

**Overall style:** Evidence-driven and methodical. Alice consistently anchors her claims to named sources, corrects factual errors with precision, and maintains composure under personal attack. Her approach is closest to rational-empirical argumentation.

**Effective tactics:**
- Appeal to evidence / citation of authority (legitimate) — "the IEA reported in 2023 that unsubsidized solar is now the cheapest source of electricity in history." — She names a specific, credible institution and year, making her claim verifiable and grounded.
- Rebuttal with counter-evidence — "Grid storage and baseload diversity solve that. Germany runs on over 50% renewables on most days now." — Rather than dismissing Bob's intermittency concern, she directly addresses the underlying technical objection.
- Distinguishing correlation from causation — "Correlation isn't causation. Their prices are high due to grid fees and taxes, not renewables themselves." — She correctly identifies a logical error in Bob's implied argument and offers an alternative causal explanation.
- Requesting substantiation — "Can you provide a source for your claims?" — A constructive Socratic tactic that forces the opposing party to either produce evidence or expose the weakness of their position.
- Deflection resistance — "Calling me uninformed doesn't address the data." — She explicitly names Bob's ad hominem as irrelevant to the substance, keeping the debate on-topic rather than getting drawn into a personal exchange.

**Problematic tactics:**
- None identified. Alice does not commit any notable logical fallacies or deploy destructive rhetorical tactics in this transcript.

**Summary:** Alice demonstrates consistently high argumentation quality. She supports every claim with identifiable evidence, addresses counterpoints on their merits, and correctly diagnoses logical errors in her opponent's reasoning. Her request for sources when challenged is a textbook constructive move that exposes the asymmetry in evidential standards between herself and Bob.

## Bob

**Overall style:** Assertive and increasingly aggressive. Bob opens with a substantive (if unsupported) counter-claim but rapidly abandons evidence-based discourse in favour of dismissal, personal attacks, and ultimately a sexist remark. His argumentation deteriorates steadily across the exchange.

**Effective tactics:**
- Initial substantive challenge — "That's only true with subsidies. Remove government support and renewables can't compete on their own." — This is Bob's only genuinely argumentative line. The subsidy dependency point is a real debate in energy economics, even if he provides no data to back it up. It introduces a legitimate variable worth examining.

**Problematic tactics:**
- Ad hominem / genetic fallacy (source dismissal) — "You can't trust the IEA, they're politically motivated." — Rather than contesting the IEA data on its merits, Bob attacks the source's character. This is a genetic fallacy: the origin or alleged motivation of a source does not determine whether its data is accurate.
- Whataboutism / topic shifting — "Besides, what about when the sun doesn't shine?" — Bob pivots away from the cost argument he raised (and could not support) to a different objection, avoiding accountability for his original unsupported claim.
- Misleading rhetorical question implying false causation — "Germany also has some of the highest electricity prices in Europe. Coincidence?" — This presents a correlation as self-evidently causal, which is precisely the logical error Alice later identifies. No mechanism or evidence is offered; the rhetorical question format is designed to make the implication feel obvious without requiring proof.
- Ad hominem (general intelligence attack) — "You just don't understand economics. Everyone with a brain knows fossil fuels are more reliable." — A double fallacy: an ad hominem dismissing Alice's competence, combined with an appeal to (unnamed, vague) consensus ("everyone with a brain") rather than any factual argument.
- Appeal to "common sense" in lieu of evidence — "I don't need sources, it's common sense." — Explicitly rejecting the evidentiary standard Alice applied throughout the discussion. "Common sense" is not an argument; it is an assertion that no argument is necessary. This is a form of bare assertion fallacy.
- Sexist ad hominem — "Women always overcomplicate things with all these statistics." — A textbook ad hominem that attacks Alice's gender rather than her argument. It also contains an overgeneralisation ("women always"). This is both a logical fallacy and a form of personal attack that has no place in substantive discourse.

**Summary:** Bob begins with a debatable but legitimate challenge and then systematically abandons rational argumentation in favour of source-dismissal, rhetorical questions, appeals to vague consensus, and personal attacks culminating in a sexist remark. His pattern of topic-shifting whenever a line of argument is challenged suggests an inability or unwillingness to defend his claims with evidence. By the end of the exchange, Bob has not provided a single verifiable source or data point, while committing at least five distinct logical fallacies.
